Unit 1

We were set up as a team to read the (Kovaitė and Stankevičienė, 2019) as the basis of our chosen case study in the collaborative discussion I, and over the next three weeks, discuss and comment on issues raised by other team members. This unit gave the team the opportunity to review and reflect on the Risk Management Process and discuss the importance of assessment approaches and user involvement in the RMP. Unit 3

Introduction to Threat Modelling and Management

In this unit, we discussed the differences between threats and vulnerabilities and got introduced to a number of threat modelling techniques, to the end of understanding which technique to be applied in specific solutions. As a team, we completed the collaborative discussion by presenting a “Summary Post”, which was a complete report on the case study under review. The lecturecast comprised of comprehensive sessions on threats and vulnerabilities, including popular threat modelling techniques such STRIDE and DREAD, Attack Trees and hybrid models such as the Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis (PASTA). Shevchenko et. al (2018) expatiated on the methods available for threat modelling in a structured manner. Each method was carefully outlined, with an historical background on the technique, data flow diagrams showing the areas of vulnerability, and a breakdown of threat stages involved in the threat modelling framework. They also concluded with a feature table to assist in making a choice of what method is best suited to what threat. This ensured that security professionals and beginners alike, can utilize the material in their day-to-day act of securing software and networks against unauthorized access and other threats. In my contribution to the Module Wiki page, the acronym CAPEC was added, which means Common Attack Pattern Enumeration and Classification. This was a term I had not come across before in my study of threat modelling. The experience of developing a summary note on the last three (3) weeks’ engagements and collaborative discussions helped us engage the learning content as well as responses from other peers, as we needed STRIDE appears to be one of the most widely used threat modelling technique and for obvious reasons; it contains a wide range of conceivable threats and vulnerabilities known in the security world. However, exposure to this unit has revealed a host of other threats that are available to unauthorized users, and also widened my knowledge on the various threat modelling techniques available to mitigate these threats. This knowledge makes it easier to protect security systems against threats and vulnerabilities. Reference Shevchenko, N., Chick, T.A., O'Riordan, P., Scanlon, T.P. and Woody, C., 2018. Unit 4

The OWASP Threat Modeling Cookbook and the ATTACK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge) libraries are valuable resources for organizations to enhance their threat modeling practices. The OWASP Threat Modeling Cookbook provides a structured approach to threat modeling, offering step-by-step guidance on how to identify, analyze, and mitigate potential threats. It includes practical examples, templates, and techniques to help organizations effectively incorporate threat modeling into their risk management processes. On the other hand, the ATTACK libraries are a comprehensive collection of known adversary behaviors, tactics, and techniques. They provide organizations with a wealth of information on how attackers operate, enabling them to identify potential vulnerabilities and design effective mitigations. The libraries cover a wide range of areas, including network attacks, web application attacks, and social engineering techniques. By leveraging these resources, organizations can gain a deeper understanding of their threat landscape, identify potential weaknesses in their systems, and implement appropriate countermeasures. This proactive approach helps organizations stay ahead of potential threats and enhance their overall security posture.

Unit 5

This unit engaged our ability to analyze industry specific security standards and how they affect risk assessments. We explored security standards such as GDPR, and how they can be incorporated in threat modelling. Case Study: Data Breach at an Online Retailer (2016) In July 2016, a report was received from an online sales organization, which showed there had been a breach of the security on the payment system of their website. This breach resulted in the fraudulent use of the credit card details of a customer. This addresses the Integrity and Confidentiality aspect of GDPR, which states that “personal data must be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of the personal data, including protection against unauthorized or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction or damage”, as well as the Accountability aspect which states that “The controller shall be responsible for, and be able to demonstrate compliance with the GDPR.” It was considered that the organization had contravened the data protection acts by failing to take appropriate security measures against unauthorized access or unauthorized alteration of its Users’ personal data, and recommendations were made to that regard. The issue was resolved when the organization put new contracts in place to ensure that appropriate technical security measures were set up on their systems, they carried out regular reviews of their server and website platforms, and also carried out annual reviews by a third party expert to ensure compliance and validate their technical security. Reference Barafort, B., Mesquida, A.L. and Mas, A., 2019. ISO 31000‐based integrated risk management process assessment model for IT organizations. Journal of Software: Evolution and Process, 31(1), p.e1984. This was a typical case study that highlighted the need for organizations to be held responsible for what happens on their platforms. Organizations should look beyond the profit they stand to gain from customers, and be actively involved in the security of customer personal data collected during transactions. This accountability will ensure there are right measures in place at all times. Personally, lessons were learned to the tone of taking responsibility for the responsible use and protection of personal data collected for transactional purposes. This case study will be referred to when discussing with clients on the need to have security measures put in place as a crucial aspect of the ecommerce system.

Unit 7

An introduction to Quantitative Risk Modelling by exploring common models to find a common ground between their functionalities, as well as identify their peculiarities in assessing risks. This was followed by a second collaborative discussion on the use of Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), which was to last for another 3 weeks, involving an initial post, review of peers’ contribution, and a final summary post. Quantitative models bring the values to the forefront and help give a mathematical backing to the need for risk management. By putting figures on risks, decision making is made easier by simple comparison of the values assigned to each vulnerability or threat identified. Being a probabilistic model, the Bayes theorem brings to mind the idea of looking beyond the technicalities, and limiting the assumptions, but quantifies the likelihood of having a threat given the measures in place or the possibilities within reach in the system. Monte Carlo is a statistical model that balances optimism and pessimism. While focusing more weight on likelihood, it gives room for exploration of the extremes in its analysis and management of risk as risk is as much as sentimental affair as it is an empirical discuss. In addition to the Multiple Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA), these are 3 powerful tools for quantifying the risk exposure of a system, to explore the management techniques that can be implemented to mitigate the risks. Reference Asadabadi, M.R., Chang, E. and Saberi, M., 2019. Are MCDM methods useful? A critical review of analytic hierarchy process (AHP) and analytic network process (ANP). Cogent Engineering, 6(1), p.1623153. Olson, D.L. & Desheng D.W (2020) Enterprise risk management models. Berlin, Germany: Springer-Verlag. Spring, J., Hatleback, E., Householder, A., Manion, A. and Shick, D., 2021. Time to Change the CVSS?. IEEE Security & Privacy, 19(2), pp.74-78. While quantitative methods give a robust and succinct analysis of risks which should make decision making easier, there exists some defects. In their critical review of the Analytic Hierarchy Process and Analytic Network Process, Asadabadi et al justified the need for further research into the validity of Multi Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) methods, as intuitive methods are sometimes easier to adopt for decision making. It was also recommended that the deficiencies of MCDM methods be analyzed as it would be a letdown to go through the analysis required to obtain values for the risk assessment, only to realize the inaccuracies in the coefficients. Risk analysis is an important subject that requires that both qualitative and quantitative measures are considered to ensure threats are checked and mitigated.

Unit 8

Following the collaborative discussion on the use of Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), from last week, we had to review the post of our peers. We also had a reading list to go through and discuss during the seminar.
Risk management is an important aspect of any project and as such, it is expected that every project team incorporates it in one form or the other. However, it is observed that risk management tools are underused, with some professionals even doubting that they are necessary (Hugo, 2018). In my experience, this could be due to the traditional way of getting things done, as projects are seen as human endeavors that have been carefully planned, various professionals consulted, and in some cases, they are simply a repetition of a previously executed project. Hence, the element of risk in projects is not considered important – in fact, Goerlandt and Reniers (2017) report that risk analysis does not always give an accurate prediction as to the outcome of the project – and when it is considered, risk management is done independently.
Furthermore, some of the risk management tools require some training before they can be properly utilized. Quantitative risk analysis tools such as the Monte Carlo simulation or Bayes, are often considered too technical for low budget/scale projects and hence, risk management is forgone all together. This shows that implementing risk management as a part of project management is subject to both human and material factors.
Hugo et al reported a correlation between overall project success and the use of quantitative management tools, which shows that risk management should be an integrated aspect of project management and not a side discuss.
Yasai (Eckstein & Riedmuller, 2002) has proven useful in simplifying risk analysis using Monte Carlo Simulation as it provides, among other functions, a mechanism for comparing scenarios autonomously. This is a tool I hope to use more often along with the spreadsheet in having a clearer understanding implementing risk assessment and management.

Unit 9

This week discusses the importance of business continuity and disaster recovery plans and provides a number of strategies that can be leveraged to address various DR requirements.
This unit introduced the business continuity (BC) and disaster recovery (DR). One of the activities to reinforce these concepts was to discuss the determining factors in BC/DR plans, explain some of the terms involved, and discuss strategies needed to meet DR requirements.
Business continuity is an organization’s ability to maintain essential functions after a disruption. The focus of business continuity is to keep the business running smoothly regardless of what disaster befalls the organization. This is a proactive business approach as it caters for eventualities before they happen.
Disaster recovery involves restoring essential data and applications to their pre-disaster state as quickly as possible. This is a reactive approach to business whereby an organization tries to savage a bad situation. The focus of disaster recovery is to restore the business after a disaster.
Andrade et al (2017) called a disaster recovery solution a business necessity in the present business world. DR can be expensive to adopt, but as an essential, it is not to be taken for granted. Disaster-Recovery-as-a-Service (DRaaS) is a cloud computing service that provides small and medium scale businesses with an affordable recovery solution in the event of a disaster.
Since BC and DR are must-have business solutions, it is important to balance the cost of implementation and solution provided. Some of the determining factors to the implementation of BCDR solutions include Business Impact Assessments (BIA), Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs).
BIA is a measure of the severity of identified risks, and how much impact they would have on an organization’s operations. RTO is the maximum time an organization’s operations can remain offline after a disruption. It is the time allowed between disaster and restoration. RPO measures the amount of data or time that an organization can afford to lose in the event of a disaster before there can be damaging effects to its business.
There is no way to decide which is of more importance between these 3 factors as they all help put together a comprehensive and functional BCDR solution. However, while RTO and RPO are factors that drive the DR plan and design, BIA helps plan ahead of disasters and also shortens the RTO in the event of a disaster. Hence, business impact assessments should take more precedence as a proper analysis could save the organization from needing damage control that DR provides.
In the future, I believe emphasis will be laid on having a BIA in place in every organization in a similar fashion to insurance schemes today. Having been exposed to some of the issues businesses are faced with in terms of damage that could be done when mechanisms are not put in place, I have learned that due diligence is required and nothing left to chance.

Reference
Andrade, E., Nogueira, B., Matos, R., Callou, G. and Maciel, P., 2017. Availability modeling and analysis of a disaster-recovery-as-a-service solution. Computing, 99, pp.929-954.

Unit 10

My team discussed some of the practical applications of Disaster Recovery and the issues that could arise during its implementation. We analyzed a number of DR solutions, depending on the organizational needs.
My years of experience have afforded me the opportunity to interact with clients and professionals in the corporate environment and I have come to understand that there is no one-size-fits-all solution to disasters. While some organizations value time more than anything else, some others can hold on for more affordable options even in the face of delay and reduced throughput. It does not make one organization have a better solution than the other, it only shows what is of value to each.
Organizations that offer delivery services, pay-as-you-go/use services would likely want a relatively low RTO when compared to organizations offering membership-based services. This hints at the specificity involved in the design and deployment of a DR solution.
Designing the DR solution revealed the essential components needed in each case study. Besides reading the theory behind the concepts, having an opportunity to draw a demo solution for different use cases showed that some implementations can be as costly as running 2 business operations concurrently. On the upside, though, it also makes switching between operations seamless in the event of a disaster.
Also, as innovative and affordable as the DRaaS is, I believe it is too much power committed to a third party, as this could result in Vendor lock-in as discussed in this unit’s activities. Vendor lock-in is an issue that arises when service providers go rogue or hold clients ransom due to unmet payment demands, violation of terms and conditions, or some other causes.

Unit 11

This unit saw the teams produce an executive summary including analysis, design, and recommendations to an organization’s digitalization project.
We carried out a quantitative risk assessment to quantify the level of potential risks the organization could be open to upon digitalization, as well as the design of a Disaster Recovery solution to prepare for possible disruption in operations.
Personally, this was a learning experience as my team mate took us through the calculation of the probability of each risk taking place and what the chances are that the organization is ready to withstand risks. We also examined the ways of mitigating the risks and developed a disaster recovery solution when disruptions eventually happen.
The requirements of the case study under discussion was such as left no room for laxity in the event of a disaster; the RTO allowed was no more than 1 minute, and so was the RPO. This translated into a concurrent running of 2 parallel systems as a preferred solution. One system on-site (active), and the other in the cloud, receiving instant updates so as to ensure the RPO stays below 1 minute, hence the concurrency.
However, for the given case study (Pampered pets), this solution appears far-fetched and unrealistic, given the resources needed to actualize it. Giving an alternative solution, though, would violate the client requirements, and that brings about the issue of opportunity cost in business.
In their paper on the bibliometric analysis and literature review of disaster response, Hou et al note that emergency response solutions are becoming more complex and uncertain (Hou et al, 2021). As a result of this future trends may tend to lean towards deterministic solutions, incorporating and building on popular solutions such as MCDM, TOPSIS, etc.
Feng and Cui (2021) also impress on the words “Optimization” and “Demand”, which is an indication of the direction to which researchers might be geared in the future.
