Unit 5

This unit engaged our ability to analyze industry specific security standards and how they affect risk assessments. We explored security standards such as GDPR, and how they can be incorporated in threat modelling. Case Study: Data Breach at an Online Retailer (2016) In July 2016, a report was received from an online sales organization, which showed there had been a breach of the security on the payment system of their website. This breach resulted in the fraudulent use of the credit card details of a customer. This addresses the Integrity and Confidentiality aspect of GDPR, which states that “personal data must be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of the personal data, including protection against unauthorized or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction or damage”, as well as the Accountability aspect which states that “The controller shall be responsible for, and be able to demonstrate compliance with the GDPR.” It was considered that the organization had contravened the data protection acts by failing to take appropriate security measures against unauthorized access or unauthorized alteration of its Users’ personal data, and recommendations were made to that regard. The issue was resolved when the organization put new contracts in place to ensure that appropriate technical security measures were set up on their systems, they carried out regular reviews of their server and website platforms, and also carried out annual reviews by a third party expert to ensure compliance and validate their technical security. Reference Barafort, B., Mesquida, A.L. and Mas, A., 2019. ISO 31000‐based integrated risk management process assessment model for IT organizations. Journal of Software: Evolution and Process, 31(1), p.e1984. This was a typical case study that highlighted the need for organizations to be held responsible for what happens on their platforms. Organizations should look beyond the profit they stand to gain from customers, and be actively involved in the security of customer personal data collected during transactions. This accountability will ensure there are right measures in place at all times. Personally, lessons were learned to the tone of taking responsibility for the responsible use and protection of personal data collected for transactional purposes. This case study will be referred to when discussing with clients on the need to have security measures put in place as a crucial aspect of the ecommerce system.

Unit 7

An introduction to Quantitative Risk Modelling by exploring common models to find a common ground between their functionalities, as well as identify their peculiarities in assessing risks. This was followed by a second collaborative discussion on the use of Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), which was to last for another 3 weeks, involving an initial post, review of peers’ contribution, and a final summary post. Quantitative models bring the values to the forefront and help give a mathematical backing to the need for risk management. By putting figures on risks, decision making is made easier by simple comparison of the values assigned to each vulnerability or threat identified. Being a probabilistic model, the Bayes theorem brings to mind the idea of looking beyond the technicalities, and limiting the assumptions, but quantifies the likelihood of having a threat given the measures in place or the possibilities within reach in the system. Monte Carlo is a statistical model that balances optimism and pessimism. While focusing more weight on likelihood, it gives room for exploration of the extremes in its analysis and management of risk as risk is as much as sentimental affair as it is an empirical discuss. In addition to the Multiple Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA), these are 3 powerful tools for quantifying the risk exposure of a system, to explore the management techniques that can be implemented to mitigate the risks. Reference Asadabadi, M.R., Chang, E. and Saberi, M., 2019. Are MCDM methods useful? A critical review of analytic hierarchy process (AHP) and analytic network process (ANP). Cogent Engineering, 6(1), p.1623153. Olson, D.L. & Desheng D.W (2020) Enterprise risk management models. Berlin, Germany: Springer-Verlag. Spring, J., Hatleback, E., Householder, A., Manion, A. and Shick, D., 2021. Time to Change the CVSS?. IEEE Security & Privacy, 19(2), pp.74-78. While quantitative methods give a robust and succinct analysis of risks which should make decision making easier, there exists some defects. In their critical review of the Analytic Hierarchy Process and Analytic Network Process, Asadabadi et al justified the need for further research into the validity of Multi Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) methods, as intuitive methods are sometimes easier to adopt for decision making. It was also recommended that the deficiencies of MCDM methods be analyzed as it would be a letdown to go through the analysis required to obtain values for the risk assessment, only to realize the inaccuracies in the coefficients. Risk analysis is an important subject that requires that both qualitative and quantitative measures are considered to ensure threats are checked and mitigated.
